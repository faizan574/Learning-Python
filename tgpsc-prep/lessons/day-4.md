## 📘 Subject 1: Indian Constitution & Polity

### Let's learn about **Citizenship (Articles 5–11, Citizenship Act 1955, CAA)**

Part II decides who belongs to India. Small in size (`Articles 5–11`), heavy in exam weight — especially with the CAA in the news.

**The constitutional articles (status at commencement, `26 Jan 1950`):**

| Article | Provision |
|---|---|
| `Art 5` | Citizenship by **domicile** at commencement |
| `Art 6` | Rights of persons who **migrated from Pakistan** to India |
| `Art 7` | Rights of persons who **migrated to Pakistan** but returned |
| `Art 8` | Citizenship of **persons of Indian origin residing abroad** |
| `Art 9` | Voluntarily acquiring **foreign citizenship** ends Indian citizenship |
| `Art 10` | Continuance of citizenship rights |
| `Art 11` | **Parliament's power** to regulate citizenship by law |

**The Citizenship Act, 1955 — five modes of acquisition:**
1. By **birth**
2. By **descent**
3. By **registration**
4. By **naturalisation**
5. By **incorporation of territory**

**Loss of citizenship (3 ways):** renunciation, termination, deprivation.

**Key features:**
- India has **single citizenship** (unlike the USA's dual federal-state citizenship).
- **OCI (Overseas Citizen of India)** is **not** dual citizenship — OCI cardholders **cannot vote, hold constitutional posts, or buy agricultural land**.
- **Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019 (CAA)**: offers a faster path to citizenship for **Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis and Christians** who entered India **on or before 31 December 2014** from **Pakistan, Bangladesh and Afghanistan** — reducing the naturalisation residence requirement. Rules were notified in **2024** (*verify latest implementation status*).

> 💎 **KEY FACT** — There are **five** modes of acquiring and **three** modes of losing citizenship — a classic "how many" MCQ.

> 🧠 **MEMORY HOOK** — Acquisition modes "**B-D-R-N-I**": **B**irth, **D**escent, **R**egistration, **N**aturalisation, **I**ncorporation.

> ⚠️ **TRAP ALERT** — Articles 5–11 only fix citizenship **at commencement**; everything afterwards is governed by the **Citizenship Act 1955** under `Art 11`. And OCI ≠ dual citizenship.

**💡 Example:** A software engineer from Hyderabad who takes US citizenship **loses** Indian citizenship automatically under `Art 9`; she may hold an **OCI card** to live and work in India, but cannot vote in Telangana elections — a direct application question.

**🎯 Exam Pointers:**
- India follows *jus sanguinis* (by descent) more than *jus soli* (by birth) after 1987 amendments.
- Citizenship is a **Union subject** (List I).
- CAA 2019 covers **6 communities, 3 countries, cut-off 31 Dec 2014**.
- NRC + CAA debates = a favourite current-affairs linkage.
- OCI merged the old PIO card scheme in 2015.
- Renunciation by one citizen affects minor children (with re-registration rights on majority).

## 📘 Subject 2: Telangana Schemes & Important Incidents

### Let's learn about **Gruha Jyothi & Indiramma Indlu**

Two guarantees that reach directly into the poor household — free power and a pucca house. Their numbers, dates and eligibility rules are prime TGPSC material.

**Gruha Jyothi (free electricity):**

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Benefit | **Zero bill** for households consuming up to **`200 units`/month** |
| Launch | **`March 2024`** |
| Eligibility | White ration card / registered eligible households |
| Design | **Threshold, not slab** — crossing `200 units` means the **entire** bill is payable |

**Indiramma Indlu (housing):**

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Benefit | **`₹5 lakh`** assistance to build a house for the eligible poor |
| Landless | Also promised a **house site** plus construction support |
| Selection | Gram Sabha + app-based survey; per-constituency targets (*verify latest official figure*) |
| Name legacy | Honours **Indira Gandhi**, continuing earlier "Indiramma" housing programmes |

> 💎 **KEY FACT** — Gruha Jyothi's `200 units` is a **cliff**: `199 units → ₹0`, `201 units → full bill`. This threshold design is the single most-asked trick of the scheme.

> 🧠 **MEMORY HOOK** — "**2-5**": Gruha Jyothi = **2**00 units free; Indiramma Indlu = ₹**5** lakh per house.

> ⚠️ **TRAP ALERT** — `200 units` free power = **Gruha Jyothi**, ₹500 LPG = **Mahalakshmi**. Different guarantees — examiners swap them.

**💡 Example:** A household using `180 units` pays nothing; its neighbour at `210 units` pays the whole bill. Under Indiramma Indlu, a landless family in a village gets both a house **site** and `₹5 lakh` to build — while an owner of a dilapidated hut gets only the construction grant. Design-comparison questions love these contrasts.

**🎯 Exam Pointers:**
- Gruha Jyothi launched March 2024, `200`-unit ceiling, white-ration-card linked.
- Indiramma Indlu = `₹5 lakh`/house; landless also get a site.
- Both target ration-card-holding poor.
- Beneficiary selection via Gram Sabha + survey app.
- "Indiramma" = Indira Gandhi legacy branding (recall the earlier Indiramma housing of the 2000s).
- Fiscal-burden vs welfare debate applies here too.

## 📘 Subject 3: Telangana History

### Let's learn about **The Chalukyas and Rashtrakutas in Telangana**

Between the Vishnukundins and the Kakatiyas, imperial dynasties from Karnataka ruled the Deccan — and left temples and a Telugu-inscription milestone in Telangana.

**Badami (Western) Chalukyas (6th–8th c. CE):**
- Capital **Vatapi (Badami)**; greatest ruler **Pulakeshin II** — checked **Harshavardhana** on the Narmada, hosted the Chinese pilgrim **Hiuen Tsang**.
- In Telangana: the **Alampur Navabrahma temples** (Jogulamba Gadwal, at the Tungabhadra–Krishna confluence) — 7th-century Chalukyan architecture (recall the Mahabubnagar map!).
- **Vemulawada Chalukyas** — a feudatory branch ruling from **Vemulawada (Rajanna Sircilla)**; patronised Jainism and Telugu-Kannada letters; the poet **Pampa** (Adikavi of Kannada) was associated with this court.

**Rashtrakutas (8th–10th c. CE):**
- Founder **Dantidurga**; capital **Manyakheta (Malkhed)** — in the Kalyani/Gulbarga region, close to Telangana's western edge.
- **Krishna I** built the monolithic **Kailasa temple at Ellora**.
- **Amoghavarsha I** — wrote **Kavirajamarga** (earliest Kannada literary work) and, per tradition, **Prashnottara Ratnamalika**; the Arab traveller **Sulaiman** ranked him among the world's great kings.
- The **Kurikyala inscription** (Karimnagar) and Jain patronage tie the Rashtrakuta age to Telangana.

**Kalyani (Western) Chalukyas / Chalukyas of Kalyani (10th–12th c. CE):**
- Revived Chalukya power from **Kalyani (Basavakalyan)**; **Someswara / Vikramaditya VI** (the *Vikrama era*).
- **Bijjala** and the rise of the **Kalachuris**; this era's collapse opened the door for the **Kakatiyas** to become independent.
- Culturally huge: **Basaveshwara's Veerashaiva (Lingayat) movement** and the *Vachana* literature emerged in this Kalyani milieu.

> 💎 **KEY FACT** — The **Alampur Navabrahma temples** are the standout Badami-Chalukya monuments **inside Telangana** — nine Shiva temples beside the Jogulamba Shakti Peetha.

> 🧠 **MEMORY HOOK** — Deccan relay: "**Badami → Rashtrakuta → Kalyani → Kakatiya**" — B-R-K-K, west-Karnataka powers handing the Deccan baton eastward to Warangal.

> ⚠️ **TRAP ALERT** — Kailasa temple at **Ellora** = **Rashtrakuta (Krishna I)**, not Chalukya; and Badami cave temples = Badami Chalukyas. Amoghavarsha wrote **Kavirajamarga** (Kannada), don't attribute it to Pampa.

**💡 Example:** At **Vemulawada**, the Rajarajeshwara temple town shows how a Chalukya feudatory seeded a lasting pilgrimage centre — history and living culture on the same site, TGPSC's favourite overlap.

**🎯 Exam Pointers:**
- Pulakeshin II vs Harsha; Hiuen Tsang's visit.
- Alampur Navabrahma = Badami Chalukya, 7th c.
- Rashtrakuta capital Manyakheta; Kailasa-Ellora by Krishna I; Amoghavarsha–Kavirajamarga.
- Kalyani Chalukyas → Vikramaditya VI (Vikrama era); Bilhana's *Vikramankadevacharita*.
- Basaveshwara & Lingayat/Vachana movement = Kalyani period.
- Kakatiyas began as Kalyani Chalukya/Rashtrakuta feudatories.

## 📘 Subject 4: Indian National Movement

### Let's learn about **Partition of Bengal, Swadeshi & the Surat Split**

The Moderate calm breaks: Curzon's 1905 partition ignites India's first mass economic movement and splits the Congress.

**Partition of Bengal (`16 October 1905`):**
- Ordered by Viceroy **Lord Curzon**; official reason "administrative convenience" (Bengal was huge); real aim — **divide Hindus and Muslims** and split the nationalist heartland.
- Created a Muslim-majority East Bengal & Assam and a Hindu-majority West.

**Swadeshi & Boycott Movement:**
- Response: use **Swadeshi** (Indian) goods, **boycott** British goods; bonfires of foreign cloth.
- **National education**: Bengal National College (Aurobindo as principal); Bengal Technical Institute.
- **Cultural mobilisation**: **Rabindranath Tagore** composed songs; **Rakhi Bandhan** on partition day symbolised unity; *Vande Mataram* became the anthem of the movement.
- Spread beyond Bengal: **Tilak** (Maharashtra), **Lala Lajpat Rai** (Punjab), **Bipin Chandra Pal** & **Ajit Singh**.

**The Extremists ("Lal-Bal-Pal") vs Moderates:**

| Feature | Moderates | Extremists |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | Reforms within British rule | **Swaraj** (self-rule) |
| Method | Petitions, prayers | Boycott, passive resistance, self-reliance |
| Icons | Gokhale, Naoroji | **Tilak, Lajpat Rai, Bipin Chandra Pal**, Aurobindo |
| Slogan | — | *"Swaraj is my birthright and I shall have it"* (Tilak) |

**The Surat Split (`1907`):**
- At the **Surat session (1907)**, Moderates and Extremists split openly over the presidency and the pace of agitation.
- Congress effectively fractured until the **Lucknow Pact (1916)** reunited them.

**Consequences:**
- **Muslim League founded (Dhaka, `1906`)** — communal politics institutionalised.
- **Morley–Minto Reforms (`1909`)** introduced **separate electorates** for Muslims.
- **Partition annulled in `1911`**; capital shifted **Calcutta → Delhi (1911)**.

> 💎 **KEY FACT** — The **Surat Split (1907)** is the exam's anchor date — Congress divided into Moderates and Extremists, weakening the movement for nearly a decade.

> 🧠 **MEMORY HOOK** — "**Lal-Bal-Pal**" = **Lal**a Lajpat Rai, **Bal** Gangadhar Tilak, Bipin Chandra **Pal** — the Extremist trio.

> ⚠️ **TRAP ALERT** — Partition of Bengal (**1905**) ≠ its annulment (**1911**) ≠ Surat Split (**1907**) ≠ Muslim League founding (**1906**). Keep the 1905–1911 sequence straight.

**💡 Example:** The **boycott of Manchester cloth** measurably dented British imports — India's first taste of economic mass struggle, the technique Gandhi would later scale nationwide. A "continuity of methods" analytical question.

**🎯 Exam Pointers:**
- Curzon partitioned Bengal; effective 16 Oct 1905; annulled 1911.
- Swadeshi's four planks: boycott, swadeshi goods, national education, cultural revival.
- Surat Split 1907; reunion at Lucknow Pact 1916.
- Muslim League 1906 (Dhaka); separate electorates 1909 (Morley-Minto).
- Tilak's newspapers: *Kesari* (Marathi), *Mahratta* (English).
- Aurobindo Ghosh — *Bande Mataram* newspaper, later spiritual turn.

- [ ] What happened at Surat in 1907?

## 📘 Subject 5: Telangana Geography (with Maps)

### Let's learn about **Climate, Rainfall Patterns & Droughts**

![Schematic map of Telangana — regions, rivers and cultural landmarks](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/faizan574/Learning-Python/main/tgpsc-prep/maps/telangana_regions.svg)
*🗺️ Read the map north→south: rainfall falls from the wetter Adilabad north to the drier Palamuru south-west.*

Why Adilabad grows cotton while Palamuru sent migrant labour — the answer is written in rainfall lines and the monsoon calendar.

**Climate type:** semi-arid to sub-humid **tropical**; hot summers (Ramagundam/Khammam among India's hottest), a monsoon wet season, and a dry winter.

**The monsoon calendar:**

| Season | Months | Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Summer | Mar–May | Very hot; loo winds; pre-monsoon thundershowers |
| **South-West monsoon** | **Jun–Sep** | Brings **~75–80%** of annual rain (main *Kharif/Vaanakalam* season) |
| Retreating/NE monsoon | Oct–Nov | Modest rain; helps *Rabi/Yasangi* |
| Winter | Dec–Feb | Dry, cool |

**Rainfall gradient (the key exam fact):**
- State average around **`900 mm`** (*verify latest normal*).
- **North & north-east (Adilabad, Kumram Bheem, Mulugu belt)**: highest, ~`1,000–1,400 mm`.
- **South-west (old Mahabubnagar/Palamuru, parts of Nalgonda)**: lowest, ~`550–700 mm` — the historic **drought-prone / rain-shadow** zone driving *Palamuru labour* migration.

**Droughts & extremes:**
- Recurrent droughts in the southern/western districts; erratic monsoon onset.
- Flip side — flash floods (e.g., the severe **Hyderabad/Musi floods**; the 2020 Hyderabad urban flood — *verify details*).
- Policy answers: **Mission Kakatiya** (tank restoration), **Rythu Bharosa/Bima**, and the big **lift-irrigation** projects (Kaleshwaram, Palamuru–Rangareddy) aimed at the dry belt.

> 💎 **KEY FACT** — Telangana gets the bulk of its rain from the **South-West monsoon (Jun–Sep)** — unlike coastal AP/Tamil Nadu, which lean on the **North-East (retreating) monsoon**.

> 🧠 **MEMORY HOOK** — "**Wet North, Dry South-West**": Adilabad drinks deep, Palamuru stays thirsty.

> ⚠️ **TRAP ALERT** — Don't say Telangana depends on the **North-East monsoon** — that's the coastal-AP/TN pattern. Telangana = **South-West monsoon** dominant.

**💡 Example:** In a weak-monsoon year, Palamuru's `~600 mm` fails first while Adilabad's cotton still gets its `~1,100 mm` — same state, two fates. Schemes and lift-irrigation are the policy response, a ready geography-plus-current-affairs question.

**🎯 Exam Pointers:**
- SW monsoon = ~75–80% of annual rain (Jun–Sep).
- Highest rainfall: Adilabad/northern belt; lowest: Palamuru south-west.
- State normal ~900 mm (*verify latest*).
- Kharif = Vaanakalam (SW monsoon); Rabi = Yasangi.
- Drought-prone core = old Mahabubnagar → hence lift schemes.
- Hottest zone: Godavari valley (Ramagundam) & Khammam.
